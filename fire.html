Overview

This was a project I worked on while interning at the USDA Forest Service. The project revolved around QUIC-Fire, a cellular-automaton-based wildfire simulator. QUIC-Fire outputs huge amounts of binary simulation data, and our job was to build an efficient visualizer for that output. Or, as efficient as one can be while dealing with hundreds of gigabytes of data. Bonus points if we could integrate VR.

Why Web-Based?

To avoid licensing issues with proprietary game engines, we decided to go with a simple web-based solution. This also meant better compatibility, and our goal was to eventually have it running on a phone. Which we did. yay!

The natural solution was A-Frame, which is built on Three.js. Three.js has been around since 2010, so it has a lot of support, which is very useful. A-Frame also supports VR - again, bonus points!

Terrain

We first read the heightmap and rendered the terrain. Then, using a clever bounding volume hierarchy raycasting add-on for Three.js called three-mesh-bvh, we allow the user to walk around on the terrain with little computation.

Trees

We used EZTree to generate the trees. We just needed something that worked. Trees could honestly be their own entire project, and making custom trees that perfectly matched QUIC-Fire's internal descriptions would have been a whole separate challenge. We did the best we could with the parameters EZTree exposed.

Fire

The next step was reading the fire data and visualizing it. This used a combination of billboarding and custom GLSL shader code. The focus was, once again, efficiency + fidelity. We could make the fire look cooler later.

Smoke

QUIC-Fire also outputs smoke data. We visualized the smoke as 2D planes arranged in blocks, which helped preserve the cellular-automaton nature of the simulation.

Wind

The wind data gets visualized as a sort of vector field. You just see arrows that point in the direction of the wind.

I've been playing video games all my life, and I never really thought about how challenging it is to visualize things that rely on senses other than our ocular ones. Wind is one of those things. You can feel it, hear it, and see what it does, but you do not really see wind itself. After iterating through many, many ideas, we ended up visualizing it as a vector field.
