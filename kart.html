Overview

This was a semester-long project where we set up Mario Kart 64 in the Math, Computer Science and Physics common room and let students play 2-player versus races throughout the semester. The goal was part data collection, part community engagement, and part excuse to make the common room way more fun.

Using BizHawk, we emulated Mario Kart 64 and ran the games through a custom Lua script I wrote. The script read values directly from emulator memory and logged race data for both players, including their characters, course, in-game time, positions, placements, items, checkpoint information, and the winner.

The semester in numbers

By the end of the semester, we had collected 1,298 completed races from 43 players, across 151 matchups, 16 courses, and 9 characters. The average race was about 2 minutes and 29 seconds, which works out to roughly 54 hours of race logs.

For this article, I anonymized everyone's usernames with random word aliases except for my own username.

Making the Data Fun

Every night, I processed the new races and posted the new rankings, as well as some other fun stats next to the setup. The rankings were computed using Dr. Roland Minton's rating system. So there was indeed some math involved! The semester ended with a tournament where the winner got a prize. I'll let you guess who won (i did).

The goal now

The current goal is to turn this dataset into a statistics and data science teaching module in the style of the SCORE Network, where students can learn from data that is familiar, competitive, and actually fun to explore.
