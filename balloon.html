Overview

This project was a team effort to build and launch a high-altitude balloon experiment. The plan was for the balloon to rise to around 30 km in the atmosphere, collect data along the way, burst at peak altitude, deploy a parachute, and then float safely back down to the ground.

The balloon launched from Green Hill Park in Salem, Virginia, and landed just east of South Boston, Virginia. It covered roughly 130 miles in just under four hours. It was only able to reach an altitude of about 27 km rather than the expected 30 km, and traveled farther than we expected. We suspect there may have been a leak in the balloon, which slowed the climb and delayed the burst.

My Contribution

Electronics and data collection

My main work was writing the code for data collection and pre-flight checks, along with putting together the electronics for the payload. Naturally, since this was a very team-heavy project, I was also involved with other team decisions and problem solving.

We used the following electronics and sensors:

  • Arduino Mega 2560 Rev3: main microcontroller.
  • DFRobot SEN0463 Geiger Counter Sensor: measured radiation levels during the flight.
  • Micro SD Card Adapter: saved the collected sensor data locally during the flight.
  • Adafruit STEMMA QT BME688: measured temperature, humidity, pressure.
  • Adafruit STEMMA QT Particulate Matter Sensor: measured PM1.0, PM2.5, and PM10 levels.
  • Adafruit STEMMA QT SCD-40: measured carbon dioxide, temperature, and humidity.
  • GT-U7 GPS Module Receiver: recorded position, altitude, speed, heading, and time data.
  • SPOT Trace: used for tracking and recovery.
  • Onboard video cameras: recorded footage during the flight.

Flight Path

Data

The electronics and data collection went mostly smoothly. The main hiccup was not code-related, we simply underestimated how much space the ropes and tie-downs would take up inside the payload. During launch, those ropes pulled one of the wires out of the Arduino and unplugged the TinyGPS connection.

Because of that, we do not have the exact position, heading, speed, and time data from the onboard GPS for the full flight. Fortunately, the SPOT Trace tracker gave us most of the flight path information we needed.
